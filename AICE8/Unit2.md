I like detective stories. Maybe you do too. They are some of the most popular forms of modern fiction. The first modern detective story, Murders in the Rue Morgue written by Edgar Allen Poe and published in  1841, features an amateur detective who solves the case without asking a single question. Ive met a few real detectives and they do ask questions. In this unit you will learn a little about how detectives and other professionals go about asking good questions. 

We've already said that children ask a lot of questions and if you have a child available then listening to the questions they ask might teach you a lot. But if we are looking at expert grownups, quite a lot of people have to be skilled in asking questions to do their job. When doctors are trying to identify what illness you might have, they may examine you and they may run tests. But they will always ask you questions. The same symptom -- say a headache -- can be caused by many different diseases. But by asking ['diagnostic questions'] doctors rule out some of them and decide what are the most likely causes. Social scientists also need to ask questions for their work. When I worked on studies of mobile phone and internet use in African countries, we developed questionnaires and interview guides. We went through a rigorous process of testing our questions to see whether people understood them, whether they led to bias, or whether they produced data in a form we could use for analysis. Police detectives get a lot of their information through questioning suspects and witnesses. Founders of start-ups and people innovating new products and services often use market research -- asking questions of people who are, or might be users of the products or services they provide or are building. So in this unit we are going to look at some of the advice that these expert questioners have to offer. 

In this unit we have assembled advice on asking questions from a wide range of expert sources.  One is from a social sciences researcher, one is from an investigative reporter, one is from a market research manual, one is from a psychiatrist, one is from a trial lawyer and one is from a training manual for police investigators. All of these professions require gathering useful and reliable information. Their reasons for asking questions are not all the same. The way they use questions differs and their advice differs. But there are also some common themes. As you read each piece of  advice, make notes: do you think the recommendations are correct? Is this advice relevant to you? How could you use the advice? What don't you understand?

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice1')">From social science researchers</button>
  <div id="advice1" class="qa-advice">
    <p>"Don’t ask leading questions or questions that are too restrictive. If you do your answers are likely to be reduced to a series of simple yes/no responses... be flexible about their responses to questions and don’t restrict them to answering your questions in the order you’ve got them in... Don’t ever give ... your opinions on the topic as this may bias them to give responses that will please you. Do remember to ask questions that probe but do not prompt a response. If a respondent gives a vague response ask them to qualify it (e.g. ‘What do you mean by that?’), but don’t give them a response to agree to (e.g. ‘Do you mean this . . . ?’)"</p>
  </div>

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice2')">From a police training manual</button>
  <div id="advice2" class="qa-advice">
    <p>"It is essential that the interviewee be provided with the opportunity to present their account before more detailed questions are asked. During this step, the interviewer should explain what they would like the first free account to cover. For suspect interviews, this will normally begin with giving the suspect the opportunity to establish their position, knowledge of, or involvement in the case. For victims and witnesses, the first free account may begin with a short statement about their initial involvement, first knowledge of, or noninvolvement in the case. This invitation should provide time as well as place and encourage the interviewee to ‘tell everything and include all details’ followed by active listening to obtain the person’s uninterrupted account. The key is to encourage the reporting of every detail, regardless of how peripheral it may seem to the main incident.”</p>
  </div>

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice3')">From a market researcher</button>
  <div id="advice3" class="qa-advice">
    <p>"There are four main types of questions: simple factual questions – requiring a yes/no responses, e.g. ‘Do you have a computer at home?’ complex factual questions – requiring some interpretation or analysis, e.g. ‘How many times have you used this application today?’ opinion and attitudinal questions – requiring more alternatives and deeper concentration open ended questions – requiring participants’ full concentration."</p>
  </div>

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice4')">From a trial lawyer</button>
  <div id="advice4" class="qa-advice">
    <p>"To get the witness to say what you want to hear, ask leading questions. If you give a witness wiggle room through open-ended inquiry, the witness will likely take advantage of it by telling the story he or she wants to convey. Put the witness on auto-pilot so you are feeding, bit by bit, the pieces of information you want the witness to acknowledge and the witness is responding with, “yes, yes, yes,” as you stand there and allow the witness to make your case."</p>
  </div>

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice5')">From an Investigative Reporter</button>
  <div id="advice5" class="qa-advice">
    <p>"Ask open-ended questions... who, what, when, where, why, and how. Avoid yes or no questions.</p>
    <p>Don’t give people easy escape routes. In other words, if you want an answer to a tough question, your subject will probably try to avoid answering it. Don’t give him an out. Don’t ask a double-barrelled question, which allows him to answer one part of your question but not the other. Avoid adding unneeded adjectives, phrases or facts that the interviewee can pick up on and run with (you know, instead of giving you a real answer).</p>
    <p>Listen, listen, listen ... Is your subject answering your question, or is she offering a tangential response? Is there some deeper meaning behind her words? Did she contradict herself? Listen, listen, listen. Sometimes the clues can be subtle."</p>
  </div>

  <button class="qa-source-btn" onclick="toggleAdvice('advice6')">From a Psychiatrist</button>
  <div id="advice6" class="qa-advice">
    <p>“Minute 1 Introduce yourself to the person, including your role in their care. Ask how they would like to be addressed. Set expectations for how much time you will meet and what you will accomplish together. Then ask: Why are you in psychiatric treatment? Minutes 2–4 Listen. A person’s uninterrupted speech displays much of their mental status, guides your history taking, and builds the alliance. Although you may be tempted to interrupt or begin asking questions, with experience you will find that allowing the person to talk initially without interruptions gives you more information than the answers to your questions. Depending on the nature of the illness, some people will be unable to fill this time; their inability to do so also reveals valuable information regarding their mental status and distress. In cases where the person does not speak spontaneously, you may have to use prompts and proceed to the history of the present illness. Remember: the clinician who is silent at first is often heard afterward.”</p>
    <p>…</p>
    <p>“You should also focus on what has changed recently—the “why now?” of the presentation. As you do, you seek understanding of precipitating events by asking questions like: When did your current distress begin? When was the last time you felt emotionally well? Can you identify any precipitating, perpetuating, or extenuating events? How have your thoughts and behaviors affected your ability to function with family, friends, and your community? How do you view your current level of functioning, and how is it different from what it was days, weeks, or months ago?”</p>
  </div>
